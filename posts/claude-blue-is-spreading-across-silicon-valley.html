Claude Blue Is Spreading Across Silicon Valley

Conversations with a Meta Senior AI Engineer and a Silicon Valley Startup Founder

In 2025, AI hit the software engineering world hard. Back then, there were two camps: those who believed coding should be entirely delegated to AI, and those who insisted humans must review everything. But here in March 2026, that debate has lost its meaning. The volume of AI-generated code has surpassed what any human could possibly review. We've been hit head-on by the transition.

I personally expected this wave to reach office workers, the non-developers, by 2026. The precedent had already been set in engineering. But when I asked about 15 people around me, working across various roles at well-known companies, not a single one had felt any bottleneck or downside from AI. Everyone said things were still at a level humans could control and manage.

Even within my own company, there was a heated internal debate. We all agreed that the era of AI fundamentally transforming white-collar work was coming. But we disagreed on timing. I thought it was still too early. I hadn't personally felt any AI-induced bottleneck at work, and neither had anyone around me. But a colleague argued that AI had already deeply changed the office landscape. People were starting to miss critical context in documents and decisions because AI-generated outputs were being passed around without sufficient understanding. And that wasn't a people problem. It was a transitional-era problem.

Then today, I had a video call with a mentor who works as a senior AI engineer at Meta, and my conviction grew stronger. In Silicon Valley, at the frontier, this situation was already reality. Hearing concrete use cases firsthand made it viscerally real. Within weeks to months, office workers will be forced into AI-native workflows too. Nobody will manually create documents, visit websites one by one, or organize notes by hand. Working through terminals, whether Claude Code or Cowork or whatever comes next, orchestrating multiple agents simultaneously. That'll be the default. And the transitional pain that hit the coding world will hit white-collar workers just the same.

What struck me even more was that he, a senior AI engineer at Meta sitting at the absolute frontier, was experiencing real existential dread from AI's progress. He'd developed a strong conviction that he needed to cultivate entrepreneurial instincts and build a business. This was someone who had been interested in startups but never imagined actually founding one himself. Now, even his side projects aren't about fun anymore. They're driven by the anxiety that the day he'll need to run his own business is approaching fast.

That same day, I had a coffee chat with a friend my age who's running a startup in Silicon Valley. A Stanford MBA who founded her company in San Francisco in 2024. She told an almost identical story. A big tech senior engineer and a startup founder, completely different positions, both feeling the same existential anxiety about AI's future. Both unable to stop. Both convinced that entrepreneurship is the only way out.

1. The AI-Native Daily Life at Meta: A World Where You Only Work with Agents

He works as a senior AI engineer at Meta. At work, he uses Claude Code. At home, he uses OpenAI's Codex. The reason is simple: at Meta, the only options are Claude Code and Gemini, and Claude is clearly better for coding. At home, he needs image generation for personal projects (which Claude Code doesn't support), and Codex Pro offers essentially unlimited tokens for $200/month. Claude Code Max is also $200, but charges extra beyond a certain usage threshold. At work, he burns through $2,000 worth of Claude Code tokens per month. That's not something you can afford personally.

What fascinated me was his work environment. He'd built his own VS Code extension that automatically generates an Obsidian knowledge graph from every AI conversation. Every interaction creates an Obsidian document, which forms links with existing notes, growing the graph over time. Obsidian used to require manual note-taking and linking. Now that AI conversations themselves are the source material, the barrier to entry has dropped dramatically. Meta has 70,000 employees; over 10,000 have joined the internal Obsidian users' group.

And every productivity tool at Meta is connected via agents. Google Drive, Docs, Sheets, email, Jira, Confluence, wikis, whiteboards. All of it. As he put it:

"I only work with agents. The results I want just accumulate in the right tools in the right format."

No need to open spreadsheets or Jira directly. Work from the terminal, and the databases populate themselves.

During his first year-end review in January, he asked the internal AI evaluation tool to "summarize everything I've done since joining." It automatically compiled every report and document he'd written into a massive list. All he had to add was his personal reflections, and the self-evaluation was done.

2. "The Entire Infrastructure Is Wrecked": The AI Code Crisis Is Real

AI adoption at Meta hasn't been all upside, though. The flip side was visible in how he described his workflow.

When he receives a new task, he spins up two agents simultaneously. One is an execution agent. He gives it the task objective and says "go, start working," even before he understands the task himself. The other is a learning agent that explains the task to him. After a day or two, the execution agent produces close to 10,000 lines of code. Meanwhile, he uses the learning agent to get up to speed, then analyzes the generated code with yet another agent. Issues he finds are mostly minor revisions, and even applying those fixes is handled by agents.

But the problem arises when people can't keep up with this speed. He was blunt:

"Meta's infrastructure is completely wrecked right now. Too many people are pushing code without reading it."

AI-generated code gets committed without adequate review, causing serious infrastructure stability issues. This is the transitional cost of the vibe coding era.

He predicted this same phenomenon would hit non-engineering roles. Imagine a company of 100 people in a non-software industry. Three people using agents could create a competing company doing the same work, undercut on price, and kill the original. Then within that team of three, the crisis of not being able to manage what agents produce would become the next problem. Companies that can't handle it would die too. Only those with the orchestration capability to manage agents with a skeleton crew would survive.

3. From Vibe Coding to Agent Orchestration

According to him, the term "vibe coding" is barely used in Silicon Valley anymore. The preferred phrase is "agent orchestration." Vibe coding was the "hey, build me a game" level of prompting. Now the approach is far more structured: detailed spec documents covering genre, character growth ranges, enemy level scaling. Everything meticulously defined. Common patterns across requests get bundled into reusable skills and tools, and how well you feed those to agents determines the quality of your output.

"Who can get AI to produce the results they actually want" has become a genuine skill. The way he explained it: going forward, the human role is about the beginning and the end. Knowing what to build. That's pure instinct. Judging whether the output is right. That's human too. And when the output falls short, being able to pinpoint exactly what's off is what separates people.

At the enterprise level, this gets messier: decomposing large tasks, deciding which parts AI can verify on its own, which need a human eye, which can proceed to the next stage without anyone checking. Since agents cost tokens, figuring out the allocation of human vs. agent resources becomes a core competency in itself.

The definition of a good developer has already shifted. Writing clean code matters less. Defining what needs to be built, and judging whether AI's output actually reflects that intent, matters more. Reading structure and purpose, not lines of code.

The same wave is coming for every knowledge worker. Probably harder. When code breaks, you get an error message. When a strategy doc misses the point, or an AI-generated analysis is subtly wrong, there's no error message. It just looks fine.

For me personally: I no longer review every cell in a spreadsheet. I ask whether this data is answering the right question. I no longer write documents from scratch. I judge whether what AI produced actually captures what we were trying to say.

Developers are already building team-level cultures around this. Shared standards for reviewing AI output, processes where humans stay in the loop at the level of intent rather than execution. Non-developers need something similar. A side project, a small team habit, anything that keeps judgment practiced and shared.

Developers got hit first and are already adapting. Studying how they're rebuilding their craft might be the most useful thing anyone in a knowledge work role can do right now.

4. Non-Engineers Are Not Exempt: Every Role at Meta

AI adoption at Meta isn't limited to engineers. Program managers use it. HR uses it. Sales uses it. Virtually every function is going all-in.

At his child's birthday party, he met a dad who works at Apple managing sales data. His entire team had transitioned to agent-based workflows, building data-driven insights for executive decision-making, all powered by agents. This freed up capacity, which they used to expand their scope.

On Meta's internal Facebook, non-engineers from HR and People teams regularly post things like: "I'm not an engineer, but I built this tool. It seems useful, give it a try." The HR team built agents that pull data from every software tool employees use, tracking what everyone is working on and creating their own evaluation scoring systems.

Even sales roles with heavy in-person meeting schedules use AI. Throw an Excel sheet with key messages at an AI tool and slides get generated automatically. He himself rarely builds slides manually anymore. Feed it your paragraphs and graphs, say "make it," iterate a few times, and you're done.

"I don't think there's anyone at Meta who isn't using AI."

5. "Oh Wow, This Thing Is Going to Replace Me": Existential Crisis at the Frontier

He earned PhDs in physics and engineering, worked at a major Asian semiconductor company and then Meta, spending years honing his expertise. So when the crisis hit, it hit someone who had real depth.

"When I write documents, I had my own methodology for structure, for visualization. That was my edge. But when I ask AI to organize something, it writes beautifully. Perfect prose, instantly."

Decades of accumulated know-how. Gone in seconds.

The timing of this crisis was precise. Until early February, he was still in the excitement phase. Every new AI feature release was met with "wow, this is amazing." But after Claude Code Opus 4.6 and Codex 5.4 dropped, everything shifted. "That 0.1 increment was the boundary where I started feeling real danger." After using the models released in mid-January more seriously in February: "Wait. I don't have much time left."

He described his psychological progression: denial, frustration, anger, and as of a few days ago, slowly shifting toward acceptance. The cycle compressed into weeks instead of months. "In the old days, you had to really use a new technology before you could evaluate it. Now I watch two hours of YouTube, try it once, and... oh, this is going to replace me. I'm done. And that happens in a single day."

What he takes the most pride in is his ability to learn. He's confident nobody can outlearn him. But against the volume agents can produce, he admitted he's losing.

6. The Base Model War Is Over: Big Tech's Reign

We eventually got to structural changes in the AI industry. His take: the base model competition is winding down, and the verdict is already in.

Only four or five companies can produce base models powerful enough to genuinely replace human-level productivity: Claude (Anthropic), GPT (OpenAI), Gemini (Google), Grok (xAI), and Meta if you count them. You can play with Chinese models like DeepSeek or Kimi as toys, but for serious, human-replacement-level work productivity, it's only these four or five.

The implication: only these five companies get to build the engineering layer for agent orchestration. With their own models, they can build agent communication structures and domain-specific value layers without worrying about margins. Companies like Perplexity that only build wrappers without a base model are inherently limited. If Google adds a bit more AI to its search, Perplexity is immediately threatened.

Meta's strategy differs slightly. Meta's AI is built for consumers to use. Instagram users scrolling through high-resolution video need something powerful yet cheap. That's a different product than Claude or GPT as B2B productivity tools. So internally, Meta has zero hesitation importing external AI tools. "The AI we build is for our customers, not for us to use."

7. Organizations That Don't Change Will Be Devoured

He previously worked at a major Asian semiconductor company. Same senior AI engineer role, but a completely different life. The key difference was "the depth of permission required to do anything." At the old company: get your boss's approval, who gets their boss's approval, then write a contract, get the contract approved through multiple rounds, prepare the work, report the preparation process. At Meta: just build it. Ship it. Tag the relevant people. "I did this. Cool with everyone?" One approval and it's live.

This difference goes beyond corporate culture. It's manufacturing culture vs. software startup culture. Legacy conglomerate plus security bureaucracy versus Meta's "build it and ship it" ethos. The combination makes the lives of people doing similar work entirely different.

On organizations that don't use AI, he was frank: "If they're not using it now, there's a reason. And they'll keep not using it." Behind the surface excuse of "security concerns" lies an interest structure. There are subsidiary companies building internal AI tools for the parent company. If someone suggests using Claude Code instead, that threatens the existing relationship. The executives won't accept that.

He used NCSoft's Lineage as an example. They hired massive AI talent, invested heavily, but the core business model of selling expensive virtual items never changed. AI features got shoved into corners nobody uses. When your success formula brought you this far, you don't change it just because something new appears.

"Eventually, companies like yours will devour these conglomerates."

8. The Next Generation's Only Weapon: Entrepreneurship

We talked about parenting. He has a young child. He believes the massive changes ahead will settle within five years. By then, his child will be in middle school, old enough to see examples of the new world and design a future accordingly. He thinks his child was born at a good time, in that sense.

But there's a prerequisite: "The next generation must have entrepreneurship. Period. Kids without a business-owner mindset won't find their place." So he's trying his best not to crush his child's spirit. Scolding kills confidence, which leads to a test-taking mentality. If a kid grows up optimized for standardized exams, the next generation offers no path for them.

At the same time, the next five years will be chaotic. For anyone in a role with a high AI displacement score: do something, anything. "This year is genuinely important. Incredibly important."

9. Silicon Valley's Two Faces: Depression and Big Dreams

By coincidence, I had a coffee chat that same day with a friend my age who's building a startup in Silicon Valley. Stanford MBA, founded a company in San Francisco in 2024, has built and killed over 40 products and counting. She was briefly visiting Korea, and what she told me sounded like a remix of the conversation I'd just had.

"Silicon Valley is genuinely very depressed right now. I even feel like leaving because of it."

Apparently, there's a term going around: "Claude Depression." The feeling that AI will eventually eliminate all human jobs and roles is bone-deep there. Meta recently laying off 16,000 developers and freezing all new graduate hiring. That's not abstract news. It's happening right next door.

This friend's company vision was "give everyone the power to develop." Bridge the gap between developers and non-developers by giving non-technical people development capabilities through AI. In 2024, that felt right. She hired nothing but software engineers besides herself. But now she worries they went all-in on one dimension and the company lacks other capabilities.

The most memorable thing she said: "You have to sell today to customers and sell ten years to investors. But we all know that in ten years, we'll all be lying in bed." Everyone around her feels the same way. This peculiar melancholy hanging over all of Silicon Valley.

And yet, she won't leave. When she first went to Stanford, she left Korea craving diversity. But looking back now, Silicon Valley is diverse in ethnicity yet everyone is in startups and AI. Ironically uniform. Still, in Korea it's hard to dream big, whereas Silicon Valley is full of crazy people with massive ambitions. That inspiration is why she wants to stay in America.

What caught me off guard was where this depression sends people's imaginations. This friend said if she were to invest, it would be in K-food. In America, Netflix always has Korean content up, and Americans are increasingly interested in K-culture, which naturally extends to Korean food featured in those shows. But Korean food in America currently has an image problem: it's not "young." She believed if you transplanted the Gangnam or Seongsu aesthetic directly to the US, it would be highly competitive. The fact that someone at AI's cutting edge sees a 30-year opportunity in an industry as far from tech as possible felt like the perfect symbol of Silicon Valley's strange mood right now.

There was an even more chilling observation. With AI eliminating jobs at scale, historically the fastest way nations have created employment is through war. Apparently, there's a half-joking, half-serious sentiment in parts of Silicon Valley: go long on defense companies, short AI startups. The optimistic scenario is that jobless people flock to VR entertainment. The pessimistic one is that nations start wars. And the fact that multiple wars have started sooner than expected makes this not entirely a joke anymore.

A senior AI engineer at Meta and a Silicon Valley startup founder. Completely different positions, big tech vs. startup, yet the emotional texture was almost identical. Dread about AI's future. An inability to stop. The conviction that entrepreneurship is the only answer. That was the air in Silicon Valley, spring 2026.

10. Relationships Are Still Something AI Can't Replace

In the AI era, everyone wants to build a global business. So do you actually need to be there in person? I asked both of them.

My mentor didn't hesitate: "Business still runs on relationships." Just the day before, he'd taken a Zoom call from a VC asking for advice. He thought he'd been fairly open and helpful. But talking with me today, he noticed the depth was different. "Building relationships is really important. But can you build a relationship over Zoom? Don't you need to share a meal?" The fact that you need to sit across from someone and eat together to truly feel comfortable. That doesn't change in the AI era.

My startup founder friend echoed the same sentiment. She described herself as an introvert who can't really do sales. Yet the way she secured early customers was through her Stanford alumni network. Stanford people are always interested in startups, so they'd often participate just because they wanted to learn too. Her reason for visiting Korea was also partly to escape Silicon Valley's AI depression, to see old friends and spend quality time with family. No matter how global and remote software work becomes, there are things only meeting people in person can fill.

Thinking about my own situation, the fact that I can casually reach out to someone at Meta and have a conversation this deep is itself an invisible asset. For most people, getting an interview with a big tech worker in America is something you'd have to pay for. These relationship assets, whether they turn into business or not, create enormous impact.

After a full day of conversations with a big tech engineer and a startup founder from Silicon Valley, one thing became clear. At the beginning of this piece, I wrote that none of the 15 people I surveyed felt any AI-induced bottleneck at work. At the time, I wondered if it was still too early. Today's conversations changed my mind. If we're not feeling the bottleneck, it's probably not because we're using AI well. It's because we're not using AI enough. Silicon Valley workers are already deep in Claude Blue. Who are we to think we're fine?

Maybe we need to push AI agents to the absolute limit, the way Silicon Valley workers do. Push until life feels wrecked, until we actually hit the bottleneck. If you're awake right now and don't have at least three agents running, your runway is burning. Time to spin up one more, starting today.

Agent orchestration is the structured practice of managing multiple AI agents to accomplish complex tasks, using detailed specifications, reusable skills, and strategic task decomposition. It replaced the earlier concept of "vibe coding," which was more casual and unstructured prompting. Agent orchestration involves deciding which tasks AI can verify autonomously, which need human review, and optimizing the allocation of human and AI resources.
