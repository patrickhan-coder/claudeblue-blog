How to Develop an Eye for Good AI Output

On the Review Paradox

At our company's weekly Thursday AI meetup, one of our AI engineers shared a line from a recent blog post by Tom Wojcik that stopped me cold:

"Developers who fully delegated to AI finished tasks fastest but scored worst on evaluations."

He called it the Review Paradox. The more you use AI, the less qualified you become to review its output.

This topic has been burning through developer communities lately. But I want to talk about the rest of us. The office workers. The non-developers.

What's left when AI does the execution?

When AI handles most of the actual execution, what's left for us? Review. Management. Planning. Strategy. Sounds impressive. But how did we learn to do those things in the first place?

We learned by doing the grunt work ourselves. By getting ripped apart by our boss at a previous job. The judgment to review someone's output exists because we've done that work hundreds of times ourselves.

How developers are fighting back

Teams are actively building systems to counter the Review Paradox. The core idea: stop reviewing code. Review the spec and architecture instead.

Before any code gets written, you write a proper spec. Define the problem clearly. Translate business language into product requirements.

Then AI writes the code, and checks whether the code follows the spec. Judging whether the spec itself is right, that's still the human job.

What this means for the rest of us

For non-developers, the shift is the same. I no longer review every cell in a spreadsheet. I ask whether the data is answering the right question.

Writing forces you to actually know your subject. In the AI era, AI can churn out blog posts. But writing by hand, even with Claude's help, is my way of keeping the review muscle alive.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the Review Paradox in AI?

The more you rely on AI to do execution work, the more your ability to judge the quality degrades.

How can knowledge workers maintain quality judgment?

Shift review from output to intent: review specs and architecture, not code or documents.
