3 Million Users With Zero Marketing Budget: The Product Is the Marketing

I had a coffee chat with a friend who, after founding a startup in Silicon Valley, built a service that now has over 3 million users globally. She's built and killed more than 40 products and is still going hard. She was briefly visiting Korea, so we caught up.

The thing that stuck with me most: in the B2C era, you need to design go-to-market and virality into the product from the very beginning. It doesn't matter how good your product is if nobody finds it. But in her case, she hit 3 million users with literally zero marketing spend. She didn't do any special marketing activities. The product itself was built to go viral.

The product is the marketing

Her first service was a casual chatbot platform. In early 2024, right after the GPT API launched, she built a service that let anyone, even non-developers, create their own chatbot. Bots that mimic a specific celebrity's speaking style, or a bot that writes in the format of a government report.

Three million cumulative users. Marketing budget: zero. The entire funnel ran on SEO alone. What happened was this: a major Korean celebrity mentioned the service on a popular talk show and demonstrated it live during a stream. That was effectively a multi-million-dollar promotion, for free. And the same viral pattern repeated in Brazil, Turkey, Portugal, India, and other non-English-speaking countries.

At the time, GPT wasn't great at capturing the cultural nuances of each language, the tone, the slang, the subtle humor. Her service nailed that. So in each country, local celebrities and influencers would find bots made in their likeness, show them off to their audiences, and the product would spread on its own.

None of this was calculated from the start. But looking back, the product's structure created the virality.

When getting a product in front of people is harder than ever, designing something that markets itself isn't a nice-to-have. It is the product strategy.

Casual is the bait. Business is the revenue.

Riding the massive traffic from the casual chatbot, she expanded into business tools. She launched a parallel service where you could drop in a Word or HWP document and have AI clean it up. The Word-based tool died within three months, because Gemini and GPT launched their own document features. But HWP (Korea's native document format) remained unsupported by AI for over two and a half years, which meant steady revenue in the Korean market. The time lag in AI adoption between the US and local markets created a real business opportunity.

The revenue structure told the whole story: 99% of revenue came from the business tools. The core users were office workers, civil servants, and teachers using it for performance reviews, internal reports, and student evaluations. The casual chatbot contributed only 1% of revenue.

But almost nobody came directly to the business tools. Everyone entered through the casual chatbot first, discovered the business features inside, and converted. The fun product was, unintentionally, the top of the funnel.

There's a deeper point here. The casual user and the business user are the same person. We're all human. We don't work 24 hours a day. The office worker who plays with a celebrity chatbot after dinner is the same person who, the next morning, needs AI to format a report. If you design for a person's whole life instead of just one use case, these funnels start to feel natural.

Throw 40 products at the wall. Keep what sticks.

Her main business now is an AI data collection and processing platform. It has a solid customer base in the US and generates stable revenue. The company's core vision, giving everyone the power to build with AI, naturally led to a data business, since good AI output requires good data input.

But she knows this won't last forever either. AI moves too fast. What works today could be obsolete tomorrow.

So she keeps building new products, launches them quietly online, watches for traction, and only invests in the ones that survive. She's built over 40 products so far. The survivors make up her current business. The rest are dead or still in development. She admitted it's getting harder to break through the noise, there are just too many AI products now, but she refuses to stop shipping.

Without go-to-market, even a great product is meaningless

AI has dropped the barrier to building products so low that anyone can ship something decent. Competition has exploded. In this landscape, thinking about marketing after building is already too late.

Her casual chatbot didn't reach 3 million users because she poured resources into marketing campaigns. It got there because the product itself was something people wanted to share.

You have to design go-to-market into the product from day one. Without that, it doesn't matter how good the thing is. The ability to spread is part of what makes a product good. That's what I walked away with today, and it's something I'll be thinking about for a long time.
